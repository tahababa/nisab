Common Mistakes

Errors to avoid when calculating and paying Zakat

Many Muslims unknowingly make errors in their Zakat calculation or distribution. Here are fifteen of the most common mistakes, along with the correct approach.

01Always Delaying to Ramadan

Many people postpone Zakat to Ramadan regardless of when their Hawl actually completes. While paying during Ramadan carries extra spiritual reward, your Zakat is due on your personal Hawl date, not on the first of Ramadan. Delaying beyond the due date is discouraged because it withholds the right of the poor.

02Forgetting Worn Gold Jewellery

In the Hanafi school, all gold and silver jewellery is zakatable, whether worn daily or stored. Many women exclude their personal jewellery from the calculation, which in the Hanafi view leaves their Zakat incomplete. Even if you follow a school that exempts worn jewellery, scholars recommend paying on it as a precaution.

03Ignoring Bank Balances

All money in bank accounts (savings, current, fixed deposits, ISAs, bonds, national savings) is zakatable. Some people only count cash in hand and forget their bank holdings.

04Excluding Trade Inventory

Business owners sometimes forget that their stock, raw materials, and work in progress are all zakatable at current market value. If you buy and sell goods, the entire inventory is subject to Zakat.

05Thinking New Income Doesn't Count

In the Hanafi school, income received even one day before your Zakat date is included in the calculation. Your Zakat date is like a financial year-end snapshot. Everything you own on that date is counted.

06Spending Zakat on Construction

Zakat cannot be spent on building mosques, schools, hospitals, or roads. The Hanafi school requires Tamlik (direct ownership transfer to an individual). You can give a poor person money, tools, or fund a business they own, but you cannot use Zakat for general infrastructure.

07Confusing Zakat with Sadaqah

Zakat is obligatory, has fixed rates, and can only go to the eight Quranic categories. Sadaqah is voluntary, any amount, to anyone. Mixing the two means your obligatory Zakat may not be fulfilled even though you have been generous.

08Not Deducting Eligible Debts

In the Hanafi, Maliki, and Hanbali schools, you should deduct immediate debts and 12 months of installment payments before calculating. Not doing so means you may overpay (which is fine as voluntary charity, but not necessary as Zakat).

09Paying a Random Amount

Some people pay an approximate amount "that feels right" without doing a proper calculation. Zakat is a precise obligation at exactly 2.5% of net zakatable wealth. Use a calculator, do the maths, and pay the correct amount.

10Using Purchase Price Instead of Market Value

Zakat is calculated on current market value on your Zakat date. For gold, use the resale value (what a gold shop would pay you), not what you paid for it. For trade goods, use today's selling price.

11Not Paying on Wife's Separate Wealth

If your wife has her own wealth above the Nisab (from earnings, inheritance, dowry, gifts), Zakat is due on it. Either she pays it herself, or you pay on her behalf with her knowledge. Her wealth is independent of yours.

12Deducting the Full Mortgage

Only 12 months of mortgage/installment payments are deductible, not the entire outstanding balance. Deducting a full £200,000 mortgage from your zakatable assets would incorrectly wipe out your Zakat obligation.

13Giving to Ineligible People

Zakat given to someone who is not genuinely eligible does not count. This is especially common when sending Zakat overseas to family members who may have non-essential assets exceeding the Nisab. Verify eligibility before paying.

14Ignoring Pension Fund Value

If you have a defined contribution or personal pension where you choose how the money is invested, the fund value is zakatable. Only defined benefit (final salary) schemes where you have no investment control are exempt.

15Paying Zakat on Haraam Income

Haraam income is not subject to Zakat. The entire amount of haraam income must be disposed of as charity (not counted as Zakat). Purify the source first, then calculate Zakat on remaining lawful wealth.

Missed previous years? If you have not paid Zakat in past years due to negligence, ignorance, or error, make a realistic estimate for each year missed and pay it as a matter of urgency. Unpaid Zakat remains as a debt upon you.
