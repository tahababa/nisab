01Setup

i Before we begin: Zakat is one of the five pillars of Islam. It is an obligation on every Muslim whose total wealth exceeds the Nisab threshold for one full hawl (lunar year). The standard rate is 2.5% of your total zakatable wealth.
The Nisab is calculated based on the value of gold or silver. Different schools of jurisprudence use slightly different gold weights to determine the threshold. This calculator fetches live prices from the Nisab Al Zakat API so your threshold is always up to date.

Can I pay Zakat in advance? Yes. Zakat covers the previous hawl period (12 hijri months), but scholars generally permit paying it in advance if you anticipate meeting the Nisab threshold.

02Cash & Bank Balances

i All cash you currently hold is zakatable, whether in your bank accounts, digital wallets, or physically at home. Include savings accounts, current accounts, fixed deposits, and any cash on hand.
Interest earned on savings accounts is a separate matter in Islamic jurisprudence, but for Zakat purposes, the total balance in your account (including any interest) should be included in your calculation. You may choose to donate the interest portion separately as sadaqah.

03Gold & Silver

i All gold and silver you own is subject to Zakat, whether it is jewellery (worn or stored), bullion, coins, or bars. This applies regardless of whether the item is for personal use or investment.
There is scholarly debate on whether frequently worn personal jewellery is exempt. The Hanafi school considers all gold and silver zakatable regardless of use. The Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali schools generally exempt regularly worn personal jewellery within customary amounts. This calculator follows the school you selected and includes all gold/silver. If you follow a school that exempts worn jewellery, you may adjust accordingly.

04Stocks & Investments

i Investments held for growth or income are generally zakatable at their current market value. This includes stocks, mutual funds, ETFs, bonds, and cryptocurrency.
For actively traded stocks (bought and sold frequently), Zakat is on the full market value. For long-term investments, some scholars say Zakat is only due on the dividend/profit portion, while others include the full value. Most modern scholars and Zakat organisations recommend using the full current market value for simplicity and caution.

Retirement accounts (pension, 401k, etc.): If accessible, include the current value. If locked until retirement, many scholars say Zakat is only due once you can access the funds, though opinions vary.

05Business & Trade Assets

i If you own a business or trade goods, the stock/inventory held for sale and the cash/receivables within the business are zakatable. Fixed assets like machinery, office furniture, or property used for the business itself are not zakatable.
Trade goods are items you purchase with the intention of reselling at a profit. Their Zakat is calculated based on their current market value (not what you paid for them). Business cash and receivables should be included here if not already counted in the Cash step.

07Debts & Deductions

iDebts and liabilities that are currently due or became due during the hawl period (12 hijri months) can be deducted from your total assets before calculating Zakat. This reduces your zakatable wealth and may bring you below the Nisab threshold.

iIslamic financing: Payments due on Sharia-compliant loans (Murabaha, Ijara, Musharaka) are deductible in full. Only include the portion due during the hawl period, not the entire outstanding balance.

Conventional (non-Islamic) loans

A note on scholarly views: There is debate among scholars on how to treat conventional (interest-bearing) loans for Zakat deduction purposes.

View 1 (majority): Deduct only the principal portion of payments due during the hawl. The interest portion is not considered a legitimate debt in Islamic law and should not reduce your zakatable wealth.

View 2 (practical): Some modern scholars allow deducting the full payment (principal + interest) since it is a real financial obligation you must meet, and failing to pay it would cause harm.

This calculator deducts the full amount you enter. We recommend consulting your local scholar for guidance specific to your situation.
